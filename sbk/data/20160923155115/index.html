ABSTRACT
Photographs are often used in educational research, particularly studies using visual arts research methodologies. The visual and conceptual relationships established between the pictures themselves in a research report are decisive for scientific and artistic quality. We recommend the use of concepts such as 'photo series', 'photo essay', 'photographic discourse', 'photo abstract', 'photo conclusion' and 'photographic quotation'. Each concept fulfils a different function and value as a specific research criterion, The article offers definitions of some of these concepts and shows photographic portraits made by students in the primary teacher education department at the University of Granada, drawing on the work of Lewis Hine.

PHOTOGRAPHY IN VISUAL ARTS-BASED EDUCATIONAL RESEARCH
    Photographs are being used extensively in visual arts-based educational research; however, explanations of the methodological criteria for their use are rare (Agra & Mesías 2007; De Lange, Mitchell and Stuart 2007; Suominen 2006, Marí n and Roldán 2008). What are the most appropriate roles for photographs in educational research reports? How do we decide what image content is relevant for a particular kind of investigation? How should we differentiate illustrative uses of photographs in educational research from contextual, narrative, argumentative or demonstrative uses?
    It is common for investigators doing photography- based educational research to employ a variety of techniques to organize their images which are not dissimilar from those used in art contexts; for example albums, portfolios, exhibitions, catalogues or collections. However, the use of models of image organization extrapolated directly from professional art leads to several problems since the purposes of educational research often involve methodological commitments that do not feature in creating art works (Bickel 2008; Irwin and Cosson 2004; Marin Viadel 2005).
    Despite the large quantity of research using photographs problems persist, especially as regards issues of validity and objectivity and the narrative and persuasive functions of images. These are evident in art education and other research disciplines that have traditionally used photographs, such as visual anthropology, visual ethnography and visual sociology. Furthermore, there is an abundance of terms related to the various uses of photographs. Some of these, like photo elicitation, photojournalism, photo montage and photo story have existed for some time; others like photo activism, photo dialogue, photo interview, photo inventory, photo poem, photo questionnaire, photo voice or photo writing are newer (Cruickshank and Mason 2003; Marshall 2007; Grady 2008; Roldán 2007).

PHOTO-EDUCATIONAL RESEARCH
    In photo-educational research the images are the most important and decisive element of the investigation. They define the problem, describe the context, provide and interpret the data, argue a case for the findings and reveal conclusions (which, in turn, may also be photos). The photographs included in a visual arts educational project combine to generate formal, narrative and conceptual interactions that are decisive in terms of their scientific and artistic interests. Thus, by taking into account traditions in the professional field of photography, we will suggest some organizational frameworks we think are valuable for educational research.

INDEPENDENT PHOTOGRAPHS
    Independent photographs do not depend either formally or conceptually on relations with other photographs. Their meaning is not substantially altered when they are published in isolation or in conjunction with other images. Since the semantic field of an independent image is open, single photographs often require an accompanying text that specifies which content is relevant for the research. One of the most common uses of independent photographs is illustration: to show examples or display elements or components of a topic under investigation. In many cases, they serve directly to present original data about a phenomenon, context or persons involved. One example is Escoles d'Altres Mons, a book by the photojournalist Kim Manresa about schools in different parts of the world (Manresa 2007).

PHOTO SERIES
    A photo series is not simply a sequence of photographs, but a congruent visual sequence, typically made by the same person or group, arranged in a formal, conceptual and narrative order. All the photographs in the series constitute a formal thematic unit.
    There are two main kinds of photographic series in educational research. The first kind is the sequence series, in which the same motif or subject appears in several places and changes or transformations are analysed. Classic examples of this from art education literature are the two photographic series in Creative and Mental Growth (Lowenfeld 1957) that show successive stages in the way a blind boy and girl modelled a human head out of clay. The second kind is the samples series, in which the same learning activity, idea or educational phenomenon is presented through a number of single cases. An example is Lewis Hine's photographic series of rural schools in the United States. Hine coined the term 'interpretive photography' (Hine c.1915) to explain his methodology. Photographic series are especially useful in descriptive and comparative studies where they provide visual evidence of analogies, similarities and contrasts.

PHOTO ESSAY
    Photo essays have been published in art education research journals for over twenty years (Corwin 1985; Stokrocki 2000). According to Fusco and McBride (in Moran 1974) Eugene Smith was the first person to write about photo essays:

A photo essayist is a photographer who manages to comprehend a subject - any subject, whether it's coal miners in Appalachia, or love, or mercury poisoning of human beings in Minamata - and gives a lot of thought to weaving the pictures into a coherent whole in which each picture has an interrelationship with the others. [...] a good essayist sometimes fights off the temptation to use a great, strong picture, because it might confuse rather than contribute to the theme.
(Moran 1974: 14-15)

    The photographs included in photo essays, and especially their interrelations, gradually reveal possible interpretations and meanings with sufficient clarity to outline the idea or line of reasoning. A photo essay may be accompanied by written text, but most importantly the critical content of the research is presented visually. Photo essays serve primarily to explain an argument because they exploit the rhetorical and narrative potential of images, not just their figurative or representational functions. Examples of photo essays in architecture education can be found on Professor Anne Whiston Spim's website at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (http://architecture.mit.eduiclass/landphoto/).

PHOTOGRAPHIC DISCOURSE
    A photographic discourse is a visually organized sequence of photos making up a complete and finished dissertation that is sufficiently elaborate, deep and complex to fulfil the requirements of educational research. They should be explicit enough to communicate everything the photographs can say and argue. A complete educational investigation based on photographs should be articulated as a photographic discourse.
    The simplest form of photographic discourse is comprised of two or more photo essays. Each essay constitutes a conceptual unit and argument but the interconnections offer a line of reasoning to the discourse. Photographic discourses can also be composed of well-considered combinations, for example of photo essays and photographic series, or photo essays and photos. A classic example is 'Photographic Analysis' in the book Balinese Character: A Photographic Analysis by Gregory Bateson and Margaret Mead (1942). In this chapter no less than 100 series, consisting of four to thirteen photographs of the human body, describe a core concept in Balinese culture: visual and kinaesthetic learning. A more recent case in point is School Days by Tomoko Sawada, which contains ten images modelled on the typical school group photograph in which students, arranged in rows, pose with their classroom teacher (Sawada 2006).

VISUAL DEFINITIONS
    This article is in effect a photo discourse that simultaneously offers three sorts of content. First, definitions of modes of photography for use in art education research; second, a description of students studying photography in teacher training schools in Andalusia; third, a method of learning photography drawing on some significant work from the history of photography.
    It begins with a single photograph (Figure 1) that functions as a photo abstract, since it synthesizes the main data and ideas of the whole photographic discourse.
    Figures 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 and 7 are independent photographs; two of which are visual quotations of work by Hine. These photographs show a classroom with students and the way the students are analysing and using Hine's works. Although these six pictures are spread over the same double page and the motifs resemble those in the rest of the article. thev do not link with each other, other than through the succession of diverse elements or details of the same theme or scene. Thus they constitute independent images despite their proximity.
    Figure 8 presents a five-image photo series. In this case, the scenery, lighting, framing, size of the human figures and other elements in the pictures share similarities. The only variation is in the features of persons portrayed in each photograph. The visual quotation from Hine, while closely related to the photographic portraits of the students, does not form a part of the series; instead, it sets out simply to explain its visual characteristics.
    Figure 10 is a basic photo essay created from two photographs that describes how the students are working. This photo essay employs a visual concept that David Hockney developed in his work called 'photo collage' (Hockney 1988).

VISUAL QUOTATIONS
    Visual quotations serve important functions similar to verbal quotations. This article demonstrates three possible ways of including them in a visual discourse. The first is the direct quotation that reproduces the image being referenced. In this case the photo being cited is faithfully represented as an independent, isolated image. Written texts use typographical devices to indicate the nature of the quotation: for example, italics or quotation marks, margins, spacing and/or font size. It is possible to use graphic design devices to indicate the presence of visual quotations. In this article we have used two such devices for this purpose: (1) a smaller-sized image just large enough to distinguish its subject matter; and (2) tone reduction. Direct quotations can be seen in Figures 4, 7, 9 and 11.
    The second device, in which the quotation is integrated into a photo, occurs when the image that is cited is an important element in the final photograph. This is the case with Figures 1, 2, 13 and 15. In all these images a photograph by Hine is clearly displayed in the hands of a person portrayed in the scene.
    The third approach is to integrate quotations into the photo essay. In this case the visual quotation is incorporated into the final image and is understood as an important element of the whole and both the formal qualities and the subject matter are used to link it to other photographs and give it coherence. The size and integrity (or fragmentation) of the original quotation depends upon qualities in the other images that make up the photo essay. This type of quotation can be seen in Figures 12, 13, 14 and 15. Copyright requirements for visual quotations must be respected. The photographs by Lewis Hine in this article are freely available for publication and have no restrictions.

CONCLUSIONS
    Each organizational framework for images we have presented serves a different art education purpose depending on the research context. But in photo-based educational studies, researchers have to organize their photographs in series and present a visual argument, preferably in the form of a photo essay or, better still, a photographic discourse. This organizational framework is desirable for two main reasons: first, because this mode of photographic thinking usually requires a specific intention and plan for the research process; and second, because it is possible to achieve visual interpretations and explanations of educational problems in this way.
